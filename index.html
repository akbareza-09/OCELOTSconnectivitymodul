OECM in the Coral Triangle

OECM is defined as a geographically defined area other than a Protected Area, which is governed and managed in ways that achieve positive and sustained long-term outcomes for the in-situ conservation of biodiversity with associated ecosystem functions and services and where applicable, cultural, spiritual, socio–economic, and other locally relevant values.

Governed & Managed

Must have a geographically defined boundary and legitimate governance.

Requires a clear geographic area with management intended for the long-term. In Raja Ampat, Sasi closures are temporary, but the governance behind them is permanent (Criterion B).
C

Effective Outcomes

Must deliver positive and sustained long-term biodiversity outcomes.

Unlike MPAs which are judged on intent, OECMs are defined by their outcomes. Recognition requires verifiable evidence that biodiversity is maintained.
D

Holistic Values

Supports ecosystem services and cultural/spiritual values.

OECMs often arise from social, economic, and cultural values (like food security or sacred sites) which are integral to the area’s success.

OECM Conservation Objectives

How conservation outcome is achieved across different management contexts.

PATHWAY 1

Primary Conservation

These sites are managed with nature conservation as the main priority, yet they remain outside the formal "Protected Area" classification. Often, governing authorities like Indigenous peoples or local communities choose the OECM framework to gain recognition for their contributions without being absorbed into state databases or government-run systems.

Examples:

* Formal managed areas for conservation (e.g., world heritage, man and the biosphere, biosphere).
* Privately managed areas for conservation (e.g., private beach for reef restoration)

* Locally Managed Marine Areas (LMMAs): Some Indigenous and community-led territories (ICCA) in Phillipines declare zones where fishing or harvesting is permanently prohibited. By reporting as OECMs, communities maintain their sovereignty while fulfilling international conservation targets.

PATHWAY 2

Secondary Conservation

In these areas, biodiversity protection is a secondary objective supporting a different primary goal. Examples include sites managed primarily for nature-based tourism or water catchment protection, where conserving the local ecosystem is essential to the success of the primary activity.

Examples:

* Customary "Tara Bandu" (Timor Leste): Customary zones in Makili and Bikeli village to regulate fishing. Fish stocks have recovered, aiding food security and eco-tourism.

* Privately managed tourism areas: restoration mangrove area as part of corporate social responsibility (CSR) program, fisheries management area from the government, etc.

PATHWAY 3

Ancillary Conservation

These sites deliver effective in situ conservation as an unintended by-product (ancillary outcome) of management, even though conservation isn't an explicit objective. This occurs in areas where human access is strictly limited for reasons like cultural taboos, historical preservation, or national security.

Examples:

* Historical War Graves (Solomons): Access restrictions to WWII wreck sites intended for historical preservation create unintended "de facto" sanctuaries where coral and fish biomass thrive undisturbed by fishing pressure.

* Military and Security Zones : Restricted coastal areas for naval exercises or defense purposes. Because these zones are closed to commercial fishing and coastal development for security reasons, they often harbor pristine reef ecosystems

* Industrial Infrastructure Safety Zones : Areas around offshore oil platforms, wind farms, or subsea cables where anchoring and fishing are prohibited for safety. These act as de facto no-take zones, providing a refuge for fish populations.

Gwala Rising in the Bwanabwana Islands

The community of Anagusa Island in Papua New Guinea is combating the effects of climate change and protecting the coral reefs they rely on using gwala: the traditional practice of setting aside a reef or forest area to allow the ecosystem to recover. Gwala is helping the community of Anagusa Island prosper - empowering men and women with improved access to food and livelihoods.
